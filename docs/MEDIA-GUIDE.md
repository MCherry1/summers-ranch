# Media Guide — Summers Ranch

Two audiences, two workflows. One section for the web developer (foundation shots with good gear), one section for Marty (day-to-day iPhone cattle documentation).

---

## For the Web Developer — Foundation Shots

These are the 10-15 images that set the tone for the entire site. Shoot them once with the Sony A6000 and the drone, then use them for years. Plan one afternoon with good light (golden hour, roughly 1-2 hours before sunset).

### The Drone Flyover (DJI Air 2S)

**What:** A slow, 60-90 second video of the property. Start high (200-300 ft) showing the full acreage, slowly descend and orbit. If the herd is grouped up, pass over them at moderate altitude. End with a wide shot of the property at sunset.

**Settings:** 4K minimum, 24 or 30fps, auto exposure. Fly slow — half stick or less. No jerky movements.

**Usage:** Hero video loop on the home page (muted, no controls, just ambiance). Also extract 3-4 still frames from the video for hero background images.

**One is enough.** You don't need multiple drone videos. One good flyover defines the property forever.

### Property Landscapes (Sony A6000 + 12mm wide)

**What:** 4-5 wide landscape shots of the ranch. Think: the view from the gate looking in, the pastures with oaks, fences running into the hills, the barn/corrals at golden hour, the property with morning fog if you can catch it.

**Settings:** f/8 for sharpness across the frame, ISO 100, tripod if you have one. Shoot RAW if you can — gives more flexibility for editing later.

**Usage:** Seasonal hero images (spring green, summer gold, fall warm, winter moody), About page backgrounds, general site imagery.

**Tips:**
- Include sky — ranch landscapes with big sky feel expansive
- Shoot the same view at different times of day if you can — morning fog vs. golden hour gives you two usable images from one spot
- Horizontal/landscape orientation only. The site uses wide banner images.

### Showcase Animal Portraits (Sony A6000 + 50mm f/1.8)

**What:** 2-3 portraits of the best-looking animals. This is the "money shot" for the cattle page — the image a buyer sees first.

**The ideal cattle portrait:** Animal standing broadside (perpendicular to you), head up and alert, ears forward, all four feet visible and square. Clean background. Even lighting — overcast days are actually perfect for this. The 50mm at f/1.8 gives you shallow depth of field that separates the animal from the background.

**Settings:** f/2.8 to f/4 (sharp but still blurred background), auto ISO, continuous autofocus. Shoot from the animal's eye level if possible — don't shoot down at them.

**Usage:** Primary card photos for featured animals. The hero-quality images that make the site look professional.

**Tips:**
- Bring a helper to position the animal and get its attention
- A whistle or feed bucket gets ears forward
- Avoid harsh midday sun — shadows under the belly and face look unflattering
- Shoot a LOT. Out of 50 frames, you'll get 2-3 great ones. That's normal.

### The 35mm f/1.8 — Your "Bring One Lens" Choice

If you don't want to carry multiple lenses, the 35mm (52.5mm equivalent) handles both the animal portraits and the wider environmental shots. You lose the creamy bokeh of the 50mm and the dramatic width of the 12mm, but you get versatility. This is the one lens to bring if you're also chasing a toddler around.

### Getting Sony Photos to the Site

The Sony → site pipeline is clunkier than the iPhone shortcut. Options:

1. **SD card → computer → drag into GitHub web UI.** Upload directly to `images/hero/` or `images/cattle/` in the browser. No pipeline needed for foundation shots — you're placing them manually.
2. **Sony Imaging Edge app → iPhone → shortcut.** Transfer wirelessly to your phone, then use the regular upload shortcut. Works but slow for large RAW files.
3. **Edit on computer first.** For the foundation shots, it's worth doing basic editing (crop, exposure, white balance) in Lightroom or even Apple Photos before uploading. These are the images that define the site's quality.

**Don't run foundation shots through the auto-resize pipeline.** Upload them at full resolution to their final locations. The pipeline is for Marty's phone photos.

## For Marty — Day-to-Day iPhone Photography

These are the photos that keep the site alive. Quick, frequent, taken during normal ranch work. No fancy equipment needed.

### The Three Shots Per Animal

When photographing cattle for the website, aim for three angles. You don't need all three every time, but over the life of an animal, try to collect:

1. **Side profile** — The most important shot for buyers. Stand at the animal's shoulder level, far enough back to see the whole body. All four feet, head up, ears forward. This is the photo that becomes the primary card image.

2. **Headshot** — Face and head filling most of the frame. Shows breed character, eye quality, and expression. Good for the "personality" feel of the card.

3. **Three-quarter angle** — Between side and front, showing depth and width. This is often the most natural photo because animals don't stand perfectly broadside for long.

### Tips for Better Phone Photos

**Light:** Early morning and late afternoon are best. Overcast days are actually ideal — soft, even light with no harsh shadows. Avoid shooting into the sun.

**Background:** Try to get a clean background behind the animal — open pasture, sky, or blurred trees. Avoid fences, equipment, or buildings cutting through the frame if you can.

**Level:** Hold the phone at the animal's eye height, not from above looking down. For calves, this might mean crouching. The animal looks more impressive when photographed at its own level.

**Ears forward:** A whistle, a shake of a feed bucket, or just a sharp noise gets those ears up. Ears forward makes the animal look alert and engaged. Ears back or to the side looks dull.

**Don't zoom.** iPhone digital zoom degrades quality fast. Walk closer instead. If you can't get close, take the shot anyway — a distant photo is better than no photo.

**Shoot multiple.** Take 3-5 photos of each animal. The shortcut lets you upload several at once for the same tag number. More photos = better selection for the primary image.

### When to Photograph

**New calves:** Within the first week. This starts the growth timeline. Even a quick phone snap of a wobbly newborn is valuable — it's the first chapter of that animal's story on the site.

**Quarterly updates:** Every 3-4 months, walk through the herd and snap a photo of each animal. The admin panel will gently remind you if an animal hasn't been photographed in a while. These updates are what make the growth timeline work — buyers can literally watch the animal develop.

**Sale prep:** Before listing an animal for sale, take a fresh side profile in good light. This is the one photo worth spending 5 extra minutes on. Walk the animal to a clean background, get someone to hold its attention, and take the shot at eye level. A good sale photo is worth more than any description.

**Anything interesting:** A cow with her newborn, the herd crossing a creek, a bull looking majestic on a hill. These become gallery content and add personality to the site. Use the "General" category in the shortcut.

### What NOT to Photograph (for the website)

- The house or specific buildings that reveal the property's layout
- License plates, street signs, or anything that pinpoints the exact location
- Anything with GPS metadata that the pipeline hasn't stripped (the pipeline handles this, but if uploading manually, strip it first)
- Veterinary procedures, medical situations, or anything unflattering

### Using the Shortcut

1. Open "Ranch Photos" on your iPhone
2. Tap **Cattle** → type the tag number → select photos of THAT animal only
3. Or tap **General** for everything else (landscapes, ranch life, gallery content)
4. Wait for the upload notification
5. Done. The pipeline handles the rest.

Photos taken in the field with no service upload fine later on WiFi. The shortcut picks from your photo library — it doesn't need cell service when you're taking the pictures.

## What the Site Needs — Content Checklist

### Must-Have (Before Sharing Publicly)

- [ ] 1 drone flyover video or 2-3 wide property landscape photos for the hero section
- [ ] At least 1 photo per animal currently in the herd
- [ ] Side profile photo of any animal listed "For Sale"
- [ ] Real phone number replacing the (209) 555-5555 placeholder

### Nice to Have (Build Over Time)

- [ ] 3+ photos per animal (builds the growth timeline)
- [ ] Seasonal landscape photos (spring green, summer gold, fall warm, winter fog)
- [ ] A few "ranch life" photos for the gallery (dogs, cats, equipment, sunsets)
- [ ] Updated photos of the brand iron on a clean background (for SVG logo trace)

### Long-Term Content Engine

The cattle cards with photo timelines are the site's unique differentiator. Every photo Marty takes of an animal adds a frame to its growth story. Over 6-12 months, each animal will have 4-8 photos showing its development from calf to mature stock. No other small ranch website does this. The value compounds over time — the more consistently Marty photographs, the richer the site becomes.

The pipeline makes this almost effortless: take photo → run shortcut → done. The site handles the rest.
